## Our Counter

### The Hidden Costs of Freelancers

```
"Freelancers can be great - I've hired them myself. But there are hidden costs people don't factor in:

**Finding & Vetting Time:**
You'll review 20+ proposals, interview 5-10 people, maybe do a test project. That's 10-20 hours of YOUR time before work even starts.

**Management Overhead:**
Freelancers need clear specs, regular check-ins, feedback loops. You become the project manager. Add another 10-15 hours.

**Risk of Failure:**
What if they disappear? What if the quality is poor? What if they're in over their head? You start over, often at twice the cost.

**No Support After:**
The project ends, they move on. Six months later something breaks - good luck getting them back.

**The Real Math:**
$50/hour freelancer × 40 hours = $2,000
Your time finding/managing: 20 hours × $100/hour = $2,000
Total real cost: $4,000+ with no guarantee

Or pay us $5,000 for a guaranteed result, with support, and get 25 hours of your life back.

Which actually costs less?"
```

## Discovery Questions to Ask

**Expose management burden:**
- "Do you enjoy managing contractors, or would you rather not?"
- "How much time do you typically spend hiring and overseeing freelancers?"
- "Who's going to write the specs and review the work?"

**Expose quality risk:**
- "Have you hired freelancers for technical work before? How did it go?"
- "What happens if the freelancer delivers something that doesn't work?"
- "How will you evaluate if the solution is built well or just 'works for now'?"

**Expose support gap:**
- "What's your plan if something breaks 3 months from now?"
- "Will the freelancer be available for questions after the project?"
- "Who maintains this once it's built?"

## Objection Handling

### "Freelancers are cheaper"

```
"The hourly rate is lower - that's true. But let's look at total cost:

Freelancer hourly: $50
Estimated hours: 40
Project cost: $2,000

Your time to hire: 10 hours × $100 = $1,000
Your time to manage: 10 hours × $100 = $1,000
Revision rounds: 5 hours × $50 = $250
No support after (risk): $???

Real cost: $4,250+ with unpredictable quality

Our project cost: $5,000
Your time: 5 hours × $100 = $500
Support included: $0
Guarantee: If it doesn't work, we keep working

Real cost: $5,500 with guaranteed results

The 'cheaper' option is $1,250 less but takes 20+ hours of your time and has no guarantee. Is that really cheaper?"
```

### "I know a good freelancer"

```
"That's valuable - having trusted people is an asset.

A few questions to consider:

Are they an AI/automation specialist, or a generalist who can figure it out?
Will they be available for support 6 months from now?
What's their track record with projects like this specifically?

If the answer to all three is strong, maybe they're the right choice.

But here's something to consider: Even great freelancers have blind spots. We bring a team with diverse expertise, battle-tested processes, and ongoing accountability.

What happens if your freelancer gets a full-time job next month?"
```

### "I've had good experiences with Upwork"

```
"That's great - it means you're good at hiring and managing.

Here's where we typically see even skilled Upwork users hit walls:

1. **Complexity** - Simple tasks work great on Upwork. Integrated automation with multiple tools? That's where freelancers often struggle.

2. **Iteration** - First version rarely works perfectly. Freelancers often treat revisions as scope creep and charge more.

3. **Long-term** - Who maintains this in 2 years? Who upgrades when tools change APIs?

We're not saying never use freelancers. We're saying: for something core to your business that needs to work reliably for years, consider whether the cheapest option is actually the best investment."
```

### "I can find specialists on Toptal/higher-end platforms"

```
"Toptal has great talent - we're not competing on raw technical skill.

The difference is:

**Toptal:** You get a skilled developer who follows YOUR specs
**Us:** You get a team that CREATES the specs based on your business

Most businesses don't fail because the developer wasn't good enough. They fail because the requirements weren't right.

We spend significant time on discovery - understanding your processes, your goals, what success looks like. Then we build.

A developer builds what you ask for.
We figure out what you SHOULD ask for, then build that.

That's a different service, and it's where the real value is."
```

### "I'll just hire a VA who knows automation"

```
"VAs are fantastic for certain things - I use them myself.

But automation is usually beyond their skillset. Here's what typically happens:

VA with 'Zapier experience' = can set up simple 2-step zaps
Your needs = multi-tool integration, error handling, edge cases, scale

They try their best, it sort of works, then breaks under real usage.
You blame them (unfair), they get frustrated, everyone loses.

Automation isn't VA-level work. It's specialist work.

The cost difference between a VA and us is significant, but so is the outcome difference. VAs are for ongoing tasks. We're for one-time system building."
```

## When to Walk Away

If they're committed to freelancer route:

```
"I respect that decision - you know your business and budget best.

Here's my honest advice if you go the freelancer route:

1. Pay for quality. The $20/hour developers will cost you more in rewrites.
2. Get detailed specs before they start - the #1 cause of failure is unclear requirements.
3. Get a support agreement in writing - what happens after delivery?

If it doesn't work out, or you want a second opinion on what they build, we're here.

Mind if I check in in a few months to see how it went?"
```

**Then**: Add to 60-day nurture. Many come back after freelancer experiences.

## Collaboration Option

Sometimes the right answer is "both":

```
"Here's a hybrid approach:

We handle the strategy and architecture - what to build and how.
A freelancer handles some of the execution under our guidance.
We handle quality control and support.

You get the cost benefit of freelancer labor with the expertise and accountability of our team.

Want me to put together what that would look like?"
```

This can work for budget-constrained prospects who genuinely can't afford full service but have strong project potential.
